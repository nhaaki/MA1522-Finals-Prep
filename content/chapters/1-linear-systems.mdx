<Section id="s1-1" chapterId="ch1" meta="§ 1.1" title="Introduction to Linear Systems">

### Key Definitions

A **linear equation** in $n$ variables has the form

$$a_1 x_1 + a_2 x_2 + \cdots + a_n x_n = b$$

where $a_1, \ldots, a_n$ are **coefficients** (known constants) and $b$ is a constant. It is **homogeneous** if $b = 0$.

A **system of linear equations** is a finite collection of linear equations. It is **homogeneous** if every $b_i = 0$.

### Solutions

- A **solution** $x_1 = c_1, \ldots, x_n = c_n$ satisfies all equations at once.
- The **general solution** captures all possible solutions.
- **Consistent**: $\geq 1$ solution exists. **Inconsistent**: no solution exists.

<Example title="A linear system in three variables">

Consider

$$\begin{cases} x_1 + 2x_2 - x_3 = 3 \\ 2x_1 - x_2 + x_3 = 1 \\ -x_1 + x_2 + 2x_3 = 4 \end{cases}$$

Each equation is linear (no products, powers, or functions of the variables). A single triple $(x_1, x_2, x_3)$ must satisfy all three simultaneously to be a solution.

</Example>

<Example title="Non-examples">

These are *not* linear equations:

$$x_1 x_2 + x_3 = 0, \qquad \sin(x_1) + x_2 = 1, \qquad x_1^2 - x_2 = 4.$$

</Example>

</Section>

<Section id="s1-2" chapterId="ch1" meta="§ 1.2" title="Solving a Linear System & Row-Echelon Form">

### Augmented Matrix

A system with coefficient matrix $A$ and constants $b$ is encoded as $[A \mid b]$:

$$\begin{cases} 2x_1 + x_2 - x_3 = 8 \\ -3x_1 - x_2 + 2x_3 = -11 \\ -2x_1 + x_2 + 2x_3 = -3 \end{cases} \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \\ -3 & -1 & 2 & -11 \\ -2 & 1 & 2 & -3 \end{array}\right]$$

### Row-Echelon Form (REF)

<Defn>A matrix is in **REF** if: (1) any zero rows are at the bottom; (2) the **leading entry** (first nonzero entry) of each row is strictly to the right of the leading entry in the row above.</Defn>

A **pivot column** contains a leading entry; a **non-pivot column** does not.

### Reduced Row-Echelon Form (RREF)

In **RREF** every leading entry is $1$ and is the only nonzero entry in its column.

<Callout tag="Key fact">Every matrix has a **unique** RREF.</Callout>

### Number of Solutions from REF/RREF

| Situation | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Last column (after bar) is a pivot column | **No solution** (inconsistent) |
| All columns of coefficient matrix are pivot columns | **Unique solution** |
| Some non-pivot column in coefficient matrix | **Infinitely many solutions** |

<Example title="Reading off consistency">

The augmented matrix below has its last column as a pivot column:

$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 5 \end{array}\right]$$

The last row says $0 = 5$ — **inconsistent**, no solution.

</Example>

</Section>

<Section id="s1-3" chapterId="ch1" meta="§ 1.3" title="Elementary Row Operations">

### Three Types

1. **Row swap:** $R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$
2. **Row addition:** $R_i + cR_j$ (add $c$ times row $j$ to row $i$, $i \neq j$)
3. **Row scaling:** $aR_j$, $a \neq 0$

<Callout tag="Key theorem">Two linear systems have the **same solutions** if their augmented matrices are **row-equivalent** (obtainable from one another by elementary row operations).</Callout>

### Reverse Operations

| Operation | Reverse |
|---|---|
| $R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$ | $R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$ (itself) |
| $R_i + cR_j$ | $R_i - cR_j$ |
| $aR_j$ | $\frac{1}{a}R_j$ |

<Example title="Applying each operation">

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_1 \leftrightarrow R_3} \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 8 & 9 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 - 4R_1} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & -3 & -6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & -3 & -6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{-\frac{1}{3}R_2} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}$$

</Example>

</Section>

<Section id="s1-4" chapterId="ch1" meta="§ 1.4" title="Row Reduction: Gaussian & Gauss-Jordan">

### Gaussian Elimination (→ REF)

1. Locate the leftmost non-zero column.
2. Swap rows so a nonzero entry sits at the top.
3. Use row addition to zero out all entries **below** the leading entry.
4. Cover the top row; repeat on the remaining submatrix.

### Gauss-Jordan Elimination (→ RREF)

Continue from REF:

5. Scale each row so the leading entry becomes $1$.
6. Work **upward**, adding multiples of each row to zero entries above each leading $1$.

<Callout tag="Note" variant="warn">REF is **not** unique; RREF is unique.</Callout>

<Example title="Full reduction">

Starting from

$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \\ -3 & -1 & 2 & -11 \\ -2 & 1 & 2 & -3 \end{array}\right]$$

After Gaussian elimination we reach REF:

$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 & 1 \end{array}\right]$$

Continuing with Gauss-Jordan gives RREF:

$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -1 \end{array}\right]$$

So $(x_1, x_2, x_3) = (2, 3, -1)$.

</Example>

</Section>

<Section id="s1-5" chapterId="ch1" meta="§ 1.5" title="More on Linear Systems">

### Summary Procedure

1. Write the system in standard form; form the augmented matrix.
2. Reduce to REF or RREF (Gaussian/Gauss-Jordan elimination).
3. Check consistency: if the last column is a pivot column → **inconsistent**.
4. Assign parameters $s, t, \ldots$ to free (non-pivot) variables.
5. Read off the general solution (directly from RREF, or by back-substitution from REF).

<Example title="Infinitely many solutions">

Suppose RREF gives

$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right]$$

Column 3 is non-pivot, so let $x_3 = t$. Then:

$$x_1 = 1 + 2t, \qquad x_2 = 4 - 3t, \qquad x_3 = t.$$

In vector form:

$$\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

</Example>

### Systems with Unknown Constants

Split into cases based on when entries vanish. For example:

$$\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} a & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & a & 1 \end{array}\right]$$

Row reducing: if $a^2 \neq 1$ there is a unique solution; if $a = 1$ the system is inconsistent; if $a = -1$ there are infinitely many solutions.

### Building a System from a General Solution

If the general solution is $(x_1, x_2, x_3) = (2 - t,\; t,\; 3)$, substituting gives $x_1 + x_2 = 2$ and $x_3 = 3$ — these two equations describe the same solution set.

</Section>
